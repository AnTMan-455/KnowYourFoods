Fried Chicken

Fried chicken is chicken coated with batter or breading and cooked in hot oil. It can be tasty and filling, but frequent intake may increase health risks due to higher calories, saturated fat, and sodium—especially when eaten with gravy, fries, or sugary drinks.

What is Fried Chicken?

Fried chicken is chicken (often thigh, drumstick, or breast) coated in flour or batter and deep-fried. The coating absorbs oil during frying, which increases total fat and calories. Fast-food versions may also be higher in sodium.

Note: Calories and sodium increase with bigger pieces, thicker breading, skin-on chicken, and added gravy/sauces.

Tip: Removing the skin and choosing grilled/air-fried chicken can lower fat intake while keeping protein.

Health Considerations

Eating fried foods too often may contribute to weight gain and can affect heart health over time. Fried chicken from fast-food restaurants may also contain high sodium, which can increase blood pressure if consumed frequently.

Balanced eating tip: If you eat fried chicken, pair it with vegetables and water, and avoid adding extra salty sauces or gravy.
